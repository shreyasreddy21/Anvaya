# JoyVerse — How to Pitch It to Institutions

*A practical playbook for getting JoyVerse in front of children who'd benefit —
schools, remedial-education centres, and clinics. Includes ready-to-use
templates.*

Your goal isn't a sale — it's a **pilot**: get a special educator to try it with
a few children, give feedback, and ideally write a short testimonial. That's a
low-stakes "yes" and a genuine resume credential.

## The mindset

- You're offering something **free** that aligns with their mission, and asking
  for their **expertise**. That's a door-opener, not a hard sell.
- Lead with **the child's benefit and your honesty**, never with features.
- Ask for **feedback, not commitment.** Small asks get yes.
- Be upfront it's **early-stage** — experts trust honesty far more than polish.

## Who to contact

Reach the **special educator / remedial therapist / head of the learning-support
cell** — not the principal or admin office. Practitioners say yes to "help your
kids + give me feedback."

Types of institutions to look for (verify each one is real and active):
- Remedial-education / learning-disability centres
- Special schools, or English-medium schools with a special-needs cell
- NGOs focused on learning disabilities
- Pediatric / child-development units at hospitals
- Local chapters of dyslexia / learning-disability associations

This field is small and runs on word of mouth — ask any special educator you
reach for a referral to others.

## The three things they'll scrutinise (be ready)

1. **Child safeguarding + the camera.** Lead with: *on-device, nothing recorded,
   off by default, opt-in.* **Demo with the camera off first.** Bring the
   parental-consent note below.
2. **Devices & internet.** Ask early: *"What devices do the children use?"* It
   needs a modern browser + internet (and a webcam only if they want expression
   sensing). If they're tablet-only or offline, scope the pilot to what works.
3. **Language fit.** JoyVerse is **English-literacy**. Pitch it as that. Target
   English-medium remediation; don't claim it helps regional-language reading.

## What to bring

- A one-page leaflet (the [SUMMARY.md](SUMMARY.md) prints well).
- [METHODOLOGY.md](METHODOLOGY.md) — signals you understand structured literacy;
  earns instant credibility with a special educator.
- A live demo on your laptop.
- The parental-consent note (below).
- After any trial: **ask for a short testimonial / letter.**

## Template: cold email to a special educator

> **Subject:** A free dyslexia-practice tool — would value your feedback
>
> Dear [Name],
>
> I'm [Your Name], a student who built **JoyVerse**, a free set of browser-based
> literacy games for children with dyslexia. It covers phonics, sight words,
> reading fluency, rapid naming and more, adapts to each child, and shows their
> progress over time. It's not a commercial product — I built it to help, and
> I'd really value the feedback of an experienced educator like you.
>
> Could I show it to you for 15–20 minutes, in person or online? I'd love your
> honest view on whether it could help the children you work with.
>
> A note on safety, since it matters: the optional expression-sensing feature
> runs entirely on the device — no video or images are ever sent or stored — and
> it's off by default.
>
> Thank you for the work you do.
>
> Warm regards,
> [Your Name] · [phone] · [email]

## Template: 5-minute demo script

1. **Frame it (30s).** "This is free, I built it to help dyslexic kids, and I'd
   love your feedback. Everything I'll show runs in a browser."
2. **Play one game (90s).** Open Phoneme Tap or Letter Sound Match. Show the
   read-aloud, the adaptive difficulty, the encouraging feedback.
3. **Show the growth story (90s).** Open **My Progress** — streaks, per-skill
   growth, "try next". Say: *"This is the point — it shows whether a child is
   improving."*
4. **Show the therapist view (60s).** "Growth at a glance" + reports — "so you
   can see progress between sessions without extra admin."
5. **Address safety (30s).** "Expression sensing is optional, on-device, nothing
   stored — here's the consent note for parents." Demo it **off**.
6. **Ask (10s).** "Would you be open to trying it with a few children and telling
   me what works and what doesn't?"

## Template: parental consent note (camera)

> **About the optional camera feature in JoyVerse**
>
> JoyVerse includes an optional feature that uses the device camera to sense your
> child's facial expressions so games can gently adapt when something feels too
> hard.
>
> - It is **off by default** and only turns on if you choose to enable it.
> - All processing happens **on your device**. **No video or images are ever
>   uploaded, sent, or stored.** Only a general mood signal (e.g. "happy",
>   "frustrated") is used while playing.
> - You can turn it off at any time in Settings.
>
> I consent / do not consent to enabling the optional camera feature for my child.
>
> Parent/guardian name: ______________  Signature: ______  Date: ______

## Honest odds (for a pilot, not a sale)

- Getting a meeting / showing it: **high** if you contact enough places.
- Getting an actual trial with children: **moderate** — gated by the three
  scrutiny points above.
- A testimonial / "we piloted this": **likely** if you're easy to work with.
- Resume value: **near-certain** — building it, pitching it, and gathering expert
  feedback is the credential, regardless of full adoption.

Go get the pilot. It's the thing that turns every other question green.

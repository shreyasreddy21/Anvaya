# JoyVerse — Overview

*A plain-language introduction. No code.*

## The problem

Dyslexia affects a large share of children — by many estimates around 1 in 10.
It is a difference in how the brain processes written language, not a lack of
intelligence or effort. The skills that need targeted practice are well known:
phonological awareness, phonics/decoding, fluency, sight-word recall, and rapid
naming. The challenge is that this practice must be **frequent, systematic, and
encouraging** — and that's hard to sustain, especially between specialist
sessions.

Two everyday frictions make it worse:
1. **Practice is repetitive and can feel discouraging**, so children disengage.
2. **Progress is hard to see**, so children, parents, and even therapists lose
   the motivating sense that things are improving.

## The approach

JoyVerse turns that practice into a set of friendly browser games, then wraps
them in the two things that make practice stick:

- **It adapts.** Difficulty adjusts to how the child is doing, and (with
  consent) the webcam reads facial expressions on-device to sense when a game
  feels too hard — gently, never punitively.
- **It shows growth.** Every session feeds a progress view that says, in plain
  terms, *"reading speed went from 48 to 63 words per minute."* That visible
  improvement is the motivation engine — for the child, the parent, and the
  therapist.

## Who it's for

- **Children with dyslexia** (roughly ages 6–12) — the players.
- **Parents** — who want to see their child improving and trust the tool is safe.
- **Therapists / special educators** — who can assign practice, monitor progress
  between sessions, and use the data to guide instruction.

## What's inside

- **12 active learning games** across the core reading-skill domains — phoneme
  tapping, letter–sound matching, letter bridging, syllable tapping, mirror/
  reversal practice (b/d/p/q), rapid naming, reading fluency, sight words,
  morphology (word building), and verbal/sequence memory.
- **A child progress dashboard** — streaks, daily goals, per-skill growth, and a
  recommended focus for what to practice next.
- **A therapist dashboard** — per-child trends, a "growth at a glance" summary,
  session history, adaptive-difficulty controls, and printable reports.
- **Accessibility built in** — a dyslexia-friendly font (OpenDyslexic),
  adjustable text size / spacing / line height, and reliable text-to-speech with
  selectable voices.
- **On-device expression sensing** — opt-in, privacy-first (see below).

## The privacy stance (important)

Expression sensing uses the webcam, but it is built to be trustworthy:

- It is **off by default** and only turns on after explicit consent.
- All processing happens **on the device, in the browser**. No video or images
  are ever uploaded or stored — only a coarse mood label is used in the moment.
- It can be turned off any time in settings.

This means JoyVerse can offer emotion-aware adaptation *without* the privacy cost
that usually comes with cameras and children.

## What JoyVerse is — and isn't

- ✅ **Is:** evidence-*aligned* structured-literacy practice with progress
  monitoring, designed to complement a specialist's instruction.
- ❌ **Is not:** a diagnostic tool. It doesn't diagnose dyslexia.
- ❌ **Is not:** a replacement for a qualified specialist.
